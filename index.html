The Michigrid concept

Michigrid is structured as a statewide network of locally governed energy cooperatives connected through shared infrastructure, shared legal pathways, and a coordinating statewide framework. The goal is not to immediately replace existing utilities. The goal is to gradually build a parallel layer of community-owned infrastructure that strengthens local economies while improving resilience and market access.

Local cooperatives

Each participating community forms its own cooperative responsible for local project development, land partnerships, community investment, and infrastructure choices suited to its geography and priorities.

  • Wind generation
  • Solar installations
  • Battery storage
  • Microgrids
  • Industrial energy partnerships

Anchor systems

Communities that cannot easily interconnect can pair local generation to anchor systems such as battery hubs, interconnection nodes, industrial campuses, or microgrid-first local loads.

  • Battery + substation hubs
  • Data and AI campuses
  • Hydrogen and industrial loads
  • Greenfleet charging depots

Statewide network

A coordinating framework helps communities share engineering knowledge, financing strategies, regulatory guidance, and large regional project planning while preserving local authority.

  • Michigrid Council
  • Development Authority
  • Technical Advisory Council
  • Shared funding and standards

Why Michigan

Michigan is unusually well positioned for community energy infrastructure. The project materials repeatedly point to a combination that few regions have all at once: Great Lakes wind potential, rural land for generation, industrial brownfields, high-voltage transmission corridors, deepwater port logistics, freshwater cooling access, and proximity to major Midwest power markets.

Wind

Strong onshore and offshore potential along the Great Lakes, especially the Muskegon–Ludington corridor.

Transmission

Access to 138 kV, 230 kV, and 345 kV corridors that matter for real interconnection and export potential.

Industrial land

Brownfields, industrial shoreline parcels, and legacy power sites create realistic infrastructure footholds.

Future industry

Energy capacity attracts AI compute, advanced manufacturing, logistics, cold storage, fleet charging, and new industrial uses.

Legal pathways and the Michigrid entity

Michigrid is designed to operate within existing energy law using pathways already used across the United States. The central legal mechanism is a nonprofit cooperative infrastructure authority that allows communities to join a statewide framework while developing local projects that can sell power into wholesale markets or pair with local anchor loads.

Core legal entity

Michigrid Cooperative Infrastructure Authority (MCIA)

  • Michigan nonprofit corporation
  • Operates with cooperative membership bylaws
  • Can create subsidiary project cooperatives and project entities
  • Holds interconnection rights, standards, and statewide project coordination

This is the legal backbone that solves the problem of smaller communities lacking independent market access.

Three practical legal routes

  • Merchant generation: produce electricity and sell it into wholesale markets through MISO.
  • Behind-the-meter / anchor load pairing: pair generation with local data, manufacturing, agriculture, logistics, or municipal loads.
  • Microgrids: operate resilient local systems that remain useful before full retail utility control is ever considered.

This lets communities start building without first taking over the local distribution grid.

Wholesale market access

Michigrid projects can operate as merchant generation facilities that produce electricity and sell it into wholesale markets rather than directly billing households at the start.

  • Day-ahead market
  • Real-time market
  • Capacity market
  • Ancillary and grid services

Community pairing framework

When communities cannot easily connect to transmission, Michigrid can pair generation to anchor infrastructure partnerships.

  • Battery hubs
  • Industrial parks
  • Microgrid systems
  • AI and compute loads

Long-term optional pathways

The model leaves room for future municipalization or cooperative distribution systems if communities later choose that route.

  • Municipal utility path
  • Cooperative expansion
  • Infrastructure acquisition later
  • Local control grows from generation first

When grid access is limited

Not every community has easy access to major transmission lines. Michigrid allows communities to move forward anyway by pairing energy generation with local electricity uses instead of making immediate export into the wider grid a requirement.

Muskegon pilot: the first Michigrid node

Muskegon County appears throughout the project materials as the strongest early pilot region. It combines industrial shoreline land, rural renewable land, port logistics, brownfield redevelopment potential, established transmission infrastructure, and proximity to some of the most strategic grid assets in the Midwest.

Industrial shoreline

Large industrial parcels and legacy power infrastructure near the port make Muskegon suitable for battery hubs, data-center power campuses, hydrogen projects, and interconnection facilities.

Rural generation land

Eastern Muskegon County contains agricultural parcels in areas such as Moorland, Ravenna, and Casnovia that fit wind leasing and utility-scale generation.

Port logistics

The Port of Muskegon offers a serious advantage for moving heavy transformers, turbine components, and major energy equipment.

Grid position

Muskegon sits near major injection points into the Midwest system, making it well suited for wholesale market participation and long-term export potential.

Solar development

Example pilot: 30 MW solar installation

  • Typical land need: 150–200 acres
  • Typical siting: brownfields, industrial land, underused acreage
  • Estimated annual production: ~45,000 MWh
  • Stable daytime generation with relatively low operating cost

Wind development

Potential townships: Moorland, Ravenna, Casnovia

  • Modern turbines: 4–6 MW each
  • Typical turbine footprint: about 1/2 acre plus access road
  • Landowner lease payments: roughly $10,000–$20,000 per turbine annually
  • Farming can often continue around turbine sites

Battery storage

Example battery pilot: 20 MW / 80 MWh

  • Stores excess solar generation
  • Balances wind output
  • Provides grid stabilization services
  • Supports microgrids and local resilience

Critical pilot partners

  • Hospitals
  • Municipal buildings
  • Water treatment facilities
  • Emergency services
  • Schools
  • Cold storage and food processing
  • Manufacturing operations
  • EV fleet charging and logistics
  • Data processing and compute centers

Strategic grid assets nearby

  • Meyer Substation: major 345 kV node energized in 2022
  • Ludington transmission hub: six 345 kV lines, ~2,292 MW pumped storage system nearby
  • Tallmadge–Kenowa corridor: 138 kV / 230 kV / 345 kV transmission route
  • Muskegon industrial lakeshore corridor: grid tie-ins, industrial parcels, port access

The likely first anchor project

The materials point toward a Michigrid Anchor Battery & Grid Hub as the strongest first physical module: a grid-connected battery and interconnection site that can sell services into wholesale markets, then allow local solar, wind, microgrids, and anchor loads to pair to it over time.

Community generation → Michigrid battery / substation hub → MISO wholesale grid → revenue → expansion into more nodes

Governance, voting, and statewide coordination

The Michigrid structure is designed to balance local control with statewide collaboration. Communities retain authority over their own projects while participating in a larger network that provides engineering support, financing strategies, technical standards, and regional coordination.

Layer 1

Statewide authority

Michigrid Cooperative Infrastructure Authority manages shared standards, anchor systems, market participation, and statewide infrastructure coordination.

Layer 2

Regional infrastructure nodes

Battery hubs, interconnection substations, industrial campuses, and energy anchor systems that interact directly with the regional grid.

Layer 3

Community cooperatives

Locally governed entities that build projects, negotiate land partnerships, shape local priorities, and decide how revenue is used in their community.

Layer 4

Project modules

Solar farms, wind projects, microgrids, battery systems, and industrial loads structured as local projects inside the broader network.

Voting structure

  • Community members: municipalities, townships, counties
  • Resident members: individuals who purchase cooperative shares
  • Infrastructure members: partners and industrial participants with advisory, not controlling, roles

Core principle: one member = one vote. Governance is not based on who puts in the most money.

Councils and boards

  • Michigrid Council: approves major infrastructure and financing decisions
  • Board of Directors: operational leadership with regional and technical representation
  • Technical Advisory Council: energy systems, grid engineering, infrastructure planning, economic development
  • Local Councils: site planning, resident participation, project oversight, and local priorities

Funding, the revenue flywheel, and community benefit

Michigrid is designed to combine multiple funding sources while building a self-reinforcing development loop. Each successful project helps fund the next one. Over time, the network becomes less dependent on new outside funding and more capable of financing its own expansion.

Funding stack

  • Federal energy grants
  • State development programs
  • Municipal revenue bonds
  • Infrastructure bank loans
  • Community investment and cooperative shares
  • Private financing and land lease partnerships
  • Corporate power purchase agreements
  • Carbon and environmental markets

Community ownership model

Residents can participate directly through cooperative memberships.

  • Community Member: $50
  • Supporting Member: $250
  • Founding Member: $1,000
  • Each member receives one vote regardless of contribution size

Long term, Michigrid can support a public energy dividend concept modeled after Alaska’s permanent fund approach, where part of system profits are reinvested and part are returned to residents.

Roadmap

The project materials describe both a practical pilot timeline and a longer statewide growth path. The early goal is not to win every fight at once. It is to get the first useful module built, generate revenue, prove governance, and create a model other communities can join.

Phase 1

Years 1–2

Form the legal entity, recruit founding leadership, organize local councils, map pilot assets, and launch feasibility, land, and interconnection work.

Phase 2

Years 2–4

Secure site control, complete engineering studies, develop the Muskegon pilot, and establish the first anchor battery / grid hub and project partnerships.

Phase 3

Years 4–7

Launch initial solar, wind, storage, and microgrid systems; begin wholesale market participation and community reinvestment.

Phase 4

Years 5–10

Expand into early network communities such as Traverse City, Marquette, Holland, Ludington, Alpena, Escanaba, Big Rapids, and Sault Ste. Marie.

Phase 5

Years 10–20

Grow regional clusters, attract industrial and compute loads, strengthen export capability, and mature into a statewide cooperative infrastructure network.

Frequently asked questions

Is Michigrid a utility company right now?

No. Michigrid is a framework for building community-owned infrastructure that can enter the energy economy through legal pathways like wholesale market participation, anchor load pairing, and microgrids before any full retail utility transition is considered.

Why not start by replacing the local utility?

Because the fastest practical move is generation first: build renewable and storage assets, sell power legally into wholesale markets, generate revenue, and expand from strength rather than starting with a direct wires-to-the-home takeover fight.

What makes Michigrid different from a normal solar proposal?

Michigrid is not one project. It is a federated system: local co-ops, shared anchor systems, shared legal and engineering support, community voting, reinvestment rules, and statewide expansion built into the structure from the start.

What is the real bottleneck?

The documents identify interconnection and transmission access as the biggest technical bottlenecks. That is why anchor battery hubs, strategic grid nodes, and paired infrastructure matter so much in the model.

Built for communities to grow as far as they want

Some communities may want modest generation that funds local resilience and reinvestment. Others may want to pursue large energy projects, AI infrastructure, industrial recruitment, electrified logistics, or long-term export strength. Michigrid is designed to support both.
