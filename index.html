Michigan • Shared Hubs • Community Ownership Pathways

Michigan can build the next energy frontier in a way that still belongs to its communities.

Michigrid is a proposed community infrastructure framework for Michigan built around a practical idea: local places need a real access point into the energy economy. Instead of forcing every community, township, regional coalition, or project sponsor to solve scale, infrastructure, storage, coordination, and long-term positioning on its own, Michigrid creates a shared hub model that local projects can pair into and grow from.

The purpose is not to make promises that outrun reality. The purpose is to build a serious platform that can start where Michigan communities actually are, help them connect to something stronger, and preserve a path toward deeper local ownership, stronger public benefit, and broader expansion as technology advances and legal opportunities evolve.

What Michigrid is

Michigrid is a community-centered energy infrastructure network designed for Michigan. It is not primarily framed as a retail electric utility, a speculative fund, or a centralized takeover model. It is structured as a cooperative infrastructure platform that helps local projects access shared hubs, storage, interconnection strategy, and wholesale market participation.

Its purpose is to help communities move from simply hosting infrastructure to actually participating in it. That means creating a structure where locally rooted projects can be coordinated through shared infrastructure, supported through practical governance, and positioned for long-term public benefit.

The problem Michigrid solves

Community energy is difficult today for structural reasons, not because communities lack ideas. Small and regional projects often run into the same barriers: high interconnection costs, transmission bottlenecks, market participation thresholds, financing challenges, and utility-centered systems that were never designed for community-scale infrastructure.

Those barriers make many otherwise viable projects too expensive or too fragmented to move forward. Michigrid is designed as the workaround. Instead of every project trying to shoulder those burdens alone, a shared infrastructure node allows multiple local efforts to connect into something stronger.

InterconnectionShared hubs reduce the burden of each project building its own market access pathway.
ScaleAggregation helps smaller projects participate in systems that are normally designed for larger players.
FinancingLayered entities make project financing more realistic than one all-or-nothing local structure.
Local leverageCommunities gain a stronger position than they would have as isolated project sponsors.
“The goal is not to wait until every law, market condition, and technology is perfect. The goal is to create a framework strong enough to begin now, useful enough to help real communities, and flexible enough to expand as Michigan gains room to move.” That is the core logic of Michigrid: a lawful, practical launch posture that still preserves the larger community-centered vision.
How it works

The operating model

Local projects begin in communities. Local cooperatives help organize participation, proposals, and local priorities. Those projects pair into a Michigrid hub that provides aggregation, storage, grid services, and interconnection strategy. From there, electricity flows into the regional wholesale market rather than requiring Michigrid to begin as a retail utility.

The hub-first strategy

The strongest current version of Michigrid begins with the anchor hub. The first phase is not “build everything at once.” It is to establish a shared regional access point with battery capacity, interconnection infrastructure, an aggregation switchyard, and supporting operations. Once that exists, surrounding community projects can connect into it instead of each trying to build a full market-access pathway alone.

The first Michigrid project

The first serious pilot is proposed for the Muskegon region because it combines several advantages in one place: industrial history, transmission proximity, brownfield opportunity, strong wind potential, rural land access, and port-linked infrastructure logic.

Legal and organizational structure

Michigrid is built around a layered legal architecture. At the top sits the Michigrid Cooperative Infrastructure Authority, a Michigan nonprofit corporation with cooperative-style membership governance. Its job is to coordinate infrastructure planning, node development, partnerships, and market participation support. It is not intended to function as a retail electric utility.

Parent entity

Infrastructure Authority

The coordinating body manages infrastructure planning, node development, partnerships, and system-wide strategy.

Protected layer

Infrastructure Trust

A trust structure is meant to hold infrastructure assets or revenue streams and keep them tied to mission-aligned uses.

Project layer

Local cooperatives and LLCs

Local cooperatives root the system in actual places while project LLCs allow individual solar, wind, and storage builds to be financed and managed cleanly.

Community ownership

Buyback pathways are part of the design

Michigrid is not just meant to “benefit” communities in vague terms. It is designed to hold open a path for real ownership. Projects can be financed with outside capital at the start, but the framework includes a community buyback mechanism that allows local cooperatives to gradually acquire larger ownership stakes over time using project revenue and structured transition rights.

Why it matters

More than symbolic participation

  • Development can happen without permanently closing communities out of ownership.
  • Long-term value has a route to remain local instead of leaving with outside control.
  • The project can move beyond generic “community benefit” language into actual ownership structure.
  • Public value, reinvestment, and stronger local stakes become part of the architecture itself.

Why this matters for Michigan

Michigrid is about more than generation alone. A strong regional energy infrastructure platform can support resilience, advanced storage, industrial redevelopment, electrified logistics, future computing loads, and other energy-intensive sectors that will increasingly shape local economies. The project is designed so that once hubs exist, they can become the backbone for broader development across Michigan.
