The Michigrid concept

Michigrid is a network of community energy cooperatives across Michigan. Each participating community can develop renewable energy infrastructure such as wind, solar, and energy storage while participating in a statewide network designed to share knowledge, resources, and long-term economic benefit.

Local cooperatives

Each community can form its own cooperative under the Michigrid network.

  • Michigrid: Muskegon Community Energy Cooperative
  • Michigrid: Detroit Community Energy Cooperative
  • Michigrid: Traverse City Community Energy Cooperative
  • Michigrid: Marquette Community Energy Cooperative

Built to scale

Each community can develop projects at its own pace while benefiting from participation in a larger system. Some may start modestly. Others may grow into major local energy and industry hubs.

What projects can include

  • Wind generation
  • Solar installations
  • Battery storage
  • Microgrid infrastructure
  • Industrial energy partnerships

Why Michigrid exists

Energy capacity is becoming one of the defining foundations of economic growth. Artificial intelligence, advanced manufacturing, electrified transportation, logistics systems, and modern digital infrastructure all require large and reliable power supply. Communities that can build practical energy capacity will be better positioned for long-term growth.

Why Michigan

  • Strong wind potential across the state
  • Large areas suitable for solar development
  • Freshwater resources important for cooling infrastructure
  • Industrial and engineering workforce
  • Transportation and logistics corridors
  • Proximity to major Midwest energy markets

Why communities matter

Michigrid is built around the idea that energy projects should not just be built in Michigan — they should be built with Michigan communities and structured so those communities can share in the value they create.

Why now

Federal and state policies are encouraging grid modernization, renewable energy development, storage systems, and resilient local energy networks. This is one of the strongest windows in decades for communities to begin building energy capacity.

Legal pathways for community energy projects

Michigrid is designed to operate within existing U.S. and Michigan energy law. The concept does not require communities to become traditional electric utilities or replace existing power companies. Instead, it uses legal project structures that are already common across the United States for independent energy development.

  • Wholesale electricity sales: renewable energy projects can sell electricity into regional markets coordinated by the Midcontinent Independent System Operator (MISO).
  • Power purchase agreements: energy can be contracted directly with large electricity users such as manufacturing facilities, logistics operations, or data infrastructure.
  • Independent generation projects: communities can develop renewable infrastructure without operating a full distribution utility.
  • Cooperative governance: residents and stakeholders can guide development through democratic cooperative structures.

These pathways allow communities to build energy infrastructure legally while gradually strengthening their economic and energy position.

When transmission access is limited

Not every community has easy access to high-capacity transmission lines. In many parts of Michigan, selling large amounts of electricity into the wider grid may be difficult or slow to permit.

Michigrid addresses this by pairing generation with local electricity demand where necessary.

  • Data processing or AI compute facilities
  • Greenhouse agriculture
  • Food processing and cold storage
  • Manufacturing operations
  • Electric vehicle fleet charging

This means communities that cannot easily export power can still use locally generated electricity productively, supporting jobs, tax revenue, and economic development while the broader network continues expanding.

How landowners and residents participate

Landowners can participate through long-term lease agreements that allow projects such as wind turbines or solar arrays to be installed on their property while maintaining productive land use.

  • Hosting wind turbines on agricultural land
  • Leasing underused acreage for solar
  • Supporting battery storage near substations or industrial land
  • Participating in community energy cooperatives

Typical turbine lease agreements in the Midwest can range from $10,000 to $30,000 per turbine per year, depending on project structure and market conditions.

Built for communities to grow as far as they want

Some communities may choose to build modest projects designed to support local resilience and generate steady reinvestment revenue. Others may pursue larger energy developments, industrial partnerships, advanced compute infrastructure, or long-term export opportunities.

Michigrid provides the building blocks, structure, and shared knowledge to support both approaches while allowing communities to decide how far they want to go.

The Muskegon Pilot Concept

The Muskegon Pilot Concept demonstrates how a Michigrid community cooperative could begin building real energy infrastructure while creating local economic value. Muskegon County offers strong wind resources, available land, industrial heritage, and transmission proximity, making it a strong candidate to demonstrate how a community energy project could realistically begin.

Solar Development

Solar is one of the easiest starting points for a pilot project. Muskegon contains several brownfield and underutilized industrial areas that could support large solar arrays.

  • Typical land requirement: 5–8 acres per MW
  • Example project: 30 MW installation
  • Estimated land: 150–200 acres
  • Estimated generation: ~45,000 MWh per year

Solar installations provide stable daytime generation and relatively low long‑term maintenance costs.

Wind Development

Eastern Muskegon County contains large agricultural parcels that could support wind turbine development while allowing farms to remain productive.

  • Modern turbines: 4–6 MW each
  • Turbine footprint: ~½ acre plus access road
  • Annual lease payments: typically $10,000–$20,000 per turbine

This creates new income streams for farmers while producing large amounts of electricity.

Potential Townships

Several eastern Muskegon County townships have characteristics that could support wind development clusters.

  • Moorland Township — large agricultural parcels and low density
  • Ravenna Township — farmland suitable for turbine leasing
  • Casnovia Township — open land capable of supporting multiple turbines

A cluster of 8–12 turbines across rural parcels could generate substantial energy while supporting local landowners.

Battery Storage & Microgrids

Energy storage systems help stabilize renewable generation and allow communities to operate critical infrastructure during outages.

  • Example battery system: 20 MW / 80 MWh
  • Stores excess solar production
  • Balances wind variability
  • Supports emergency microgrid operations

Microgrids could support hospitals, water systems, schools, and municipal buildings during grid disruptions.

Anchor Industry Opportunities

Renewable generation can support local economic development by powering facilities that require large and reliable electricity supply.

  • Data processing or AI compute facilities
  • Greenhouse agriculture
  • Cold storage and food processing
  • Manufacturing operations
  • Electric vehicle fleet charging hubs

Community Economic Impact

  • Construction employment during build‑out
  • Long‑term technical maintenance jobs
  • New revenue for farmers through turbine leases
  • Expanded township tax base
  • Funding for community programs

Energy infrastructure can become a long‑term revenue engine that helps support local priorities.

Purpose of the Pilot

The Muskegon project is designed as a demonstration of how communities across Michigan could build their own energy capacity. Lessons learned from the pilot could help guide similar projects in other towns across the state.

Over time, each participating community could contribute to a larger Michigrid network.

The Michigrid revenue flywheel

Michigrid is designed to create a long-term development cycle.

Build energy infrastructure
Wind, solar, and storage projects create electricity capacity.

Generate revenue
Electricity can be sold into regional energy markets or used to power local industry.

Reinvest locally
Revenue can fund community priorities and future projects.

Expand the network
New communities join the system and build additional infrastructure.

Example allocation model

  • 40% — infrastructure expansion
  • 30% — community development
  • 20% — member dividends
  • 10% — Michigrid expansion fund

This creates a self-reinforcing development model where each successful project helps build the next one.

Adaptive technology strategy

Traditional infrastructure projects often lock in technology for decades. Michigrid is designed to evolve. Early projects build the financial and technical capacity needed for future upgrades and new technologies.

Community benefits

Energy projects can support broader community goals such as housing revitalization, healthcare access, education and workforce training, broadband expansion, and community infrastructure improvements.

Why now is the right time

Major grants, financing structures, and policy support for renewable generation, storage, grid modernization, and resilient local energy systems are already in place. That makes this a strong time for communities to begin exploring projects like these.

What Michigrid Could Look Like in 20 Years

The long‑term vision of Michigrid is not a single project, but a growing network of community energy cooperatives across Michigan. As more communities develop renewable generation, storage, and local energy partnerships, the collective impact could significantly strengthen Michigan’s energy capacity and economic resilience.

Growing Community Participation

Over time, communities across the state could form their own local cooperatives using the Michigrid framework.

  • Michigrid: Muskegon Community Energy Cooperative
  • Michigrid: Detroit Community Energy Cooperative
  • Michigrid: Traverse City Community Energy Cooperative
  • Michigrid: Marquette Community Energy Cooperative
  • Michigrid: Alpena Community Energy Cooperative

Each community would remain locally governed while benefiting from shared knowledge and collaboration across the network.

Potential Statewide Capacity

If dozens of communities participate, Michigan could gradually build a significant amount of new renewable generation.

  • 30–50 participating communities
  • 5–10 GW combined renewable capacity
  • Major wind development across rural regions
  • Solar on brownfields and industrial land
  • Battery storage supporting grid stability

This capacity could strengthen Michigan’s energy independence and support major future industries.

Economic Impact

  • Billions in long‑term infrastructure investment
  • New construction and technical jobs
  • Stable income for rural landowners
  • Expanded local tax bases
  • Funding for community development programs

Over time, successful projects could even position Michigan to export electricity to surrounding states during periods of surplus generation.

The goal of Michigrid is not to replace existing utilities overnight — it is to help Michigan communities gradually build the energy capacity, infrastructure, and economic strength needed for the future.

How Communities Could Join Michigrid

Michigrid is designed as an open framework that communities across Michigan could adopt if they choose to begin developing local energy infrastructure. The process is intentionally flexible so that towns of different sizes can participate at their own pace.

Step 1 — Community Organization

  • Form a local working group
  • Explore cooperative structure
  • Identify community priorities
  • Begin outreach with residents and landowners

Step 2 — Resource Assessment

  • Evaluate wind and solar potential
  • Identify brownfields or underused land
  • Map transmission access
  • Explore potential anchor industries

Step 3 — Pilot Project Development

  • Secure land lease agreements
  • Conduct engineering and feasibility studies
  • Apply for grants and financing
  • Develop first renewable project

Once operational, each cooperative could share knowledge and experience with the broader Michigrid network to help other communities begin their own projects.

Frequently asked questions

Is Michigrid a utility company?

No. It is a public-facing infrastructure concept and long-term framework for community-centered energy development in Michigan.

Can communities participate without immediately selling into the broader grid?

Yes. Projects can power local facilities directly, which means towns with limited transmission access can still use generation productively and create revenue locally.

Does every town have to do the same thing?

No. Michigrid is intentionally flexible. Each community can decide how ambitious it wants to be and what mix of projects best fits its needs.

Why call it a pilot concept?

Using “pilot concept” keeps the site honest and transparent. It avoids implying permits or funding already exist while still showing how a real project could develop.

Michigrid is a conceptual framework for community energy development and infrastructure planning. Specific projects would operate within applicable federal, state, and regional energy regulations.
